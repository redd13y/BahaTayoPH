Flood Control & Accountability

When flood-control funds vanish into ghost projects and kickbacks, communities pay for the failure twice — once when the water rises, and again when the infrastructure meant to stop it was never really built.

How It Works

The Anatomy of a Flood Control Scam

Senate Blue Ribbon Committee inquiries have exposed an elaborate kickback system built on two interlocking mechanisms — one in the budget, and one in the bidding room.

Budget Manipulation
✂️

Budget Insertions

Legislators, abusing their mandate to review the National Expenditure Program, divert funds to flood-control projects in their own districts — often to companies they own or are linked to.

📈

Ballooning Appropriations

Flood-control budgets grew from ₱79 billion in 2016 to over ₱200 billion annually by 2021, then funded 9,855 projects worth over ₱545 billion between mid-2022 and mid-2025.

🏢

Executive-Level Complicity

Accountability extends to appropriations officials — from the Department of Budget and Management to the Department of Finance — who allegedly received campaign donations from favored contractors.

Rigged Procurement
🤝

Kickback Hierarchy

Contractors are pressured toward illicit payments to secure favored status — a chain of commissions running from district engineers up to top DPWH officials and lawmakers, who reportedly take a 25–30% cut.

👻

Ghost & Substandard Projects

Favored contractors — protected by district engineers, lawmakers, and executive officials — deliver ghost projects or shoddy work, leaving flood infrastructure unbuilt, unfinished, or unsafe.

🕳

Captured Oversight

Procurement boards, auditors, public banks, and regulatory agencies whose complicity or negligence allowed anomalous funds to be laundered are also part of the accountability chain.

Source: Weena Gera, Urban Studies Program, UP CIDS. Flood-Control Fiasco: A Policy Reckoning for Accountability in the Philippines' Climate Risk Governance. October 2025. cids.up.edu.ph

🌡

This isn't just theft — it's climate injustice.

Every peso lost to corruption erodes the country's capacity to adapt, prepare, and protect its citizens. Corruption in flood control is a climate governance failure, not just a fiscal one.

Following the Money

Timeline of the Flood Control Scandal

From street protests to a Senate probe, a fatal fall, and a rejected billion-peso bribe — how the investigation has unfolded.

September 21, 2025
The "Trillion Peso March"
Public indignation over anomalous flood-control spending boils over into nationwide protests, three days before a major storm makes landfall.
September 24, 2025
Report Names PH Most Disaster-Prone
The 2025 World Risk Report names the Philippines the world's most disaster-prone country the same day Severe Tropical Storm Opong makes landfall, flooding communities nationwide.
Late 2025
Senate Blue Ribbon Inquiry
Hearings expose a kickback system linking contractors, district engineers, DPWH officials, and lawmakers — and testimonies alleging billions in insertions and payoffs.
December 18–19, 2025
Ex-DPWH Official Found Dead
Former DPWH Undersecretary Maria Catalina "Cathy" Cabral — accused of a central role in fund manipulation — is found unconscious near the Bued River in Tuba, Benguet, and later pronounced dead.
December 19–20, 2025
"The Wheel of Justice Will Not End"
DILG Secretary Jonvic Remulla vows the probe continues regardless of Cabral's death; an autopsy attributes it to blunt trauma consistent with a fall, with no signs of foul play.
January 9, 2026
₱1 Billion Bribe Offer Revealed
Remulla discloses that he and his brother, Ombudsman Jesus Crispin Remulla, were allegedly offered ₱1 billion through an intermediary to stop the investigation — and refused.
January 2026
Assets Frozen, More Cases Filed
The AMLC freezes over ₱20 billion in illegally acquired wealth, expected to reach ₱100 billion, as authorities pursue extradition of officials wanted for graft and file further cases over ghost projects.

The Path Forward

What Real Accountability Requires

Policy analysts argue that fixing flood control means treating corruption as a climate governance issue — not just a fiscal one.

🔬

Science-Based Budgeting

Hazard and risk mapping from institutions like Project NOAH and the UP Resilience Institute should inform appropriations, subject to independent technical review.

🔓

Open, Participatory Budgets

Institutionalizing an "open bicam" and transparent budget deliberations can check arbitrary insertions before they become anomalous flood-control projects.

🖥

Digital Procurement

Digitalized contracting paired with independent, citizen-led engineering audits can verify that resilience infrastructure is actually being built.

⚖️

Independent Institutions

Reform must strengthen constitutional bodies — the Commission on Audit, the Ombudsman, and the judiciary — rather than rely solely on ad hoc commissions vulnerable to political capture.

🏛

Prosecuting the Highest Levels

Accountability must reach legislators, DPWH officials, and executive-level appropriators — not only the contractors who carried out the schemes.

📢

Sustained Public Pressure

History shows scandals can fade without follow-through. Citizen oversight and continued public attention are what keep investigations from stalling.

Calamities are not just natural and climate change phenomena — they are moral indictments of state failure.
