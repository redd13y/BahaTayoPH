Understanding Floods
in the Philippines

Why the country remains vulnerable — and what geography, climate, and policy all have to do with it.

Section 1

Why the Philippines Is Flood-Prone

A combination of geography, weather patterns, and location makes the Philippines one of the world's most disaster-exposed nations.

🌏

Archipelagic Geography

7,641 islands surrounded by ocean mean long coastlines exposed to storm surges and sea-level rise from every direction.

🌬

Monsoon Seasons

Habagat (southwest monsoon) brings heavy rains from June–October. Amihan (northeast) causes floods in eastern areas November–February.

🌀

Typhoon Belt

The Philippines sits in one of the world's most active typhoon zones — averaging 20 typhoons per year, 8–9 of which make direct landfall.

🏘

Coastal Communities

Over 60% of Filipinos live in coastal areas, many in informal settlements on floodplains with little protection or early-warning access.

🏔

Deforestation

The Philippines has lost over 90% of its original forest cover, removing natural barriers that absorb rainfall and prevent runoff.

🏙

Urban Sprawl

Rapid urbanization has replaced natural drainage with concrete, causing rainwater to surge into streets and settlements instead of soaking into the ground.

Section 2

Causes of Flooding in the Philippines

According to the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA), flooding in the Philippines stems from both natural phenomena and human-made factors.

Natural Causes
🌧

Meteorological Events

Intense and prolonged rainfall spells are a primary trigger. Unusually high coastal and estuarine water levels caused by storm surges and seiches also contribute significantly to flooding along coastlines.

🌀

Tropical Cyclones & Monsoons

The Philippines experiences an average of 20 typhoons per year. These, in combination with the Habagat (southwest monsoon) or Amihan (northeast monsoon), can bring extreme and sustained rainfall capable of overwhelming river systems and drainage networks.

🌊

Seismic Activities

Tsunamis (seismic sea waves), the sinking of land due to earthquakes, and the uplifting of lake and river beds from seismic causes can all result in the overflowing of rivers and bodies of water.

🌕

Astronomically-Influenced Phenomena

High tides coinciding with heavy rainfall events can worsen coastal flooding — an increasing concern as sea levels rise due to climate change.

Human-Made Causes
🌲

Deforestation of Watersheds

The denudation of forest and watershed areas alters the ecological system of river basins. With over 90% of original forest cover lost, rainwater rushes directly into rivers instead of being absorbed — drastically increasing flood risk.

🗑

Garbage & Clogged Drainage

Throwing garbage in esteros and rivers — including plastic wrappers — clogs drainage systems. PAGASA explicitly identifies this as a preventable human contribution to flooding that communities can directly address.

🏗

Construction & Infrastructure Failures

Construction of temporary dams can impede river flow and cause overflow. Failures of dikes, levees, and other hydraulic structures — or their mismanagement — can release enormous volumes of water into populated areas.

💥

Blasting & Land Disturbance

Blasting in hilly or mountainous areas can cause landslides that unintentionally dam rivers and streams — leading to sudden upstream flooding and eventual catastrophic release.

💧

Minor Flooding

Inundation due to accumulation of excessive surface run-off in low-lying areas. Floodwaters are usually shallow and confined to the river's flood plain.

🌊

Major Flooding

Caused by rivers and lakes overflowing, breaks in dikes and levees, or uncontrollable releases from reservoirs. Covers wide areas rapidly and can be deeply destructive.

⚡

Flash Floods

Develop in 6 hours or less — typically in hilly and mountainous terrain with steep river slopes. Extremely dangerous due to sudden onset with little warning time.

Source: Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA) — DOST. Floods: Causes, Types, and Safety. Learning Tools. Retrieved July 2025 from pagasa.dost.gov.ph/learning-tools/floods

Section 3

Climate Change Is Making It Worse

As global temperatures rise, the flood risks the Philippines already faces are being amplified — with measurable, devastating consequences.

A Warming Planet

Every 1°C rise in global temperature intensifies rainfall and strengthens typhoons.

🌀

Stronger Storms

Warmer ocean surfaces fuel more intense typhoons. Super Typhoon Yolanda (2013) reached wind speeds of 315 km/h — among the strongest ever recorded at landfall.

🌊

Rising Sea Levels

Philippine seas are rising 3× faster than the global average — up to 14mm per year in some areas. This worsens storm surges and saltwater intrusion into coastal communities.

🌧

Unpredictable Rainfall

Climate change disrupts monsoon patterns, causing prolonged droughts followed by extreme rainfall events — overwhelming drainage systems never designed for such volumes.

🌡

Urban Heat Islands

Cities like Metro Manila trap heat, intensifying local rainfall. Combined with impermeable surfaces, this causes flash floods even from moderate rain events.

Section 4

Timeline of Major Flood Events

A look at the deadliest and most destructive floods to hit the Philippines — and the communities still rebuilding.

September 2009
Typhoon Ondoy (Ketsana)
464 deaths
₱11 billion in damages
Dumped a month's worth of rain in 6 hours over Metro Manila. Marikina River rose 6m above flood level. Nearly 1 million displaced.
December 2011
Typhoon Sendong (Washi)
1,257 deaths
₱2.5 billion in damages
Flash floods and mudslides devastated Cagayan de Oro and Iligan City in Mindanao. Struck at midnight, catching residents asleep.
November 2013
Super Typhoon Yolanda (Haiyan)
6,300+ deaths
₱571 billion in damages
The strongest tropical cyclone ever to make landfall. A 5–6m storm surge obliterated Tacloban City. 4 million people displaced.
October 2015
Typhoon Lando (Koppu)
58 deaths
₱7 billion in damages
Slow-moving typhoon dumped record rainfall over Central Luzon. Cagayan Valley flooded for weeks, destroying rice harvests and displacing 216,000 people.
November 2020
Typhoon Ulysses (Vamco)
101 deaths
₱12.4 billion in damages
Struck during pandemic lockdowns. Floodwaters reached rooftops in Marikina and Cagayan Valley — the worst flooding in decades for many communities.
December 2021
Super Typhoon Odette (Rai)
375 deaths
₱35 billion in damages
Devastated Visayas and Mindanao just before Christmas. Siargao, Bohol, Cebu, and Surigao saw catastrophic flooding and storm surges. 500,000+ homes damaged.
2024–Present
Escalating Flood Events
Hundreds annually
Billions per event
Increasingly frequent flooding with shorter recovery windows. Communities in NCR, Cagayan Valley, and Bicol report annual floods with rising intensity — a pattern consistent with climate projections.
📢

These are not isolated events — they are a pattern.

Every flood on this timeline exposed the same gaps: inadequate drainage, poor land-use policy, underprepared communities, and delayed government response.
